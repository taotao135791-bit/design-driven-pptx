# Education & Training

> Applicable scenarios: Early childhood education courseware (kindergarten/elementary), secondary education courseware, higher education courseware (university courses), student classroom presentations, corporate training (onboarding/safety/skills, etc.), educational lectures, etc.
> Style anchor: Quality open course/TED-Ed educational style, balancing knowledge delivery efficiency with learning experience

## Design Philosophy

- **Audience profile drives everything**: First identify who the audience is (age, knowledge level, attention characteristics), then determine density, color, font size, language style, and all other design parameters
- **Cognitively friendly**: Follow cognitive load theory; information volume matches the audience's cognitive capacity, avoiding both overload and excessive sparsity
- **Progressive**: Knowledge points unfold gradually with setup, transitions, and summaries, following the learning curve
- **Visuals aid understanding**: Diagrams, illustrations, and flowcharts are not decoration — they are tools for lowering comprehension barriers
- **Interactive feel**: Maintain learner attention through questions, whitespace, comparisons, case studies, and other techniques

## Universal Education & Training Characteristics

Regardless of the audience, all education and training scenarios share these core features:

- **One topic per page**: Each page focuses on one knowledge point or skill; the title is the page's conclusion
- **Titles carry knowledge points**: Page titles summarize the core knowledge point rather than vague chapter names
- **Visuals as comprehension tools**: All diagrams must aid understanding; purely decorative images are prohibited
- **Knowledge unfolds with rhythm**: Introduce problem/phenomenon → Concept explanation → Examples/cases → Summary/practice
- **Projection readability**: All color schemes and font sizes must be clearly readable in projection/large-screen environments

## Scenario-Specific Notes

### Student Classroom Presentations

The core goal of student classroom presentations is to clearly present research/learning outcomes and persuade the audience (teachers and classmates).

- **Logic first**: Clear "problem-method-results-conclusion" structure or "argument-evidence-summary" structure
- **Simple and restrained**: Flashy design is unnecessary; clear content and tidy layout suffice. Avoid over-decoration that distracts attention
- **Data/evidence support**: Core arguments accompanied by charts, data, citations, and other supporting materials
- **Time awareness**: Classroom presentations are usually time-limited; moderate information per page to avoid cramming too much to cover
- **Color suggestions**: Choose 1 primary + 1 secondary color for visual unity
- **Common layout**: Cover (title + name + date) → Table of contents → Background/problem → Method/process → Results/data → Discussion/conclusion → References

### Corporate Training

The core goal of corporate training (onboarding, safety training, skills training, etc.) is to help trainees quickly master policies, processes, or skills and execute them in practice.

- **Brand integration**: Use corporate brand colors as the theme color; cover/chapter pages should reflect corporate identity
- **Practice-oriented**: Focus on processes and steps; use numbered lists and flowcharts to display operational procedures
- **Information layering**: Must-know (policy red lines/safety baselines) → Should-know (process standards) → Optional (advanced resources), with clear priority
- **Case-driven**: Replace abstract regulations with real-world scenario cases to lower comprehension barriers
- **Right vs. wrong comparison**: Side-by-side display of correct vs. incorrect practices for intuitive standards communication
- **Checklists**: Key process pages paired with checklist-style bullet points for trainees to follow along
- **Prominent warning information**: For safety, compliance, and other critical information, use high-contrast colors (red/orange) + bold + large font to ensure visibility
- **Short and direct language**: Each rule/step stated in one sentence, imperative tone ("must," "strictly prohibited"), avoiding long paragraphs

## Information Density: Medium to Medium-High (60-80%)

- Overall fill rate **60-80%**, flexibly adjusted based on audience age and cognitive level
- Early education (kindergarten/elementary) leans toward lower density (40-60%); 1-2 knowledge points per page; whitespace gives learners room to digest
- Secondary education uses moderate density (60-70%); 1-2 knowledge points per page; can accommodate more text explanation
- Higher education and corporate training lean toward higher density (65-80%); each page can carry richer information layers
- Key concepts visually emphasized (large text, color blocks, icons); supporting information in smaller text
- Avoid dense text walls; paragraphs exceeding 6 lines should be split across pages or converted to diagrams

> **Principle: After viewing a page, the learner should be able to summarize what the page covered in one sentence**

### Density Reference

The following describes typical page compositions at each density level based on a 1280x720 canvas:

- **Low-density page (early education)**: Page title (conclusion sentence) + one large diagram (built with shapes/lines/text, occupying 50-60% of the page) + 2-3 brief bullet points + moderate whitespace. Visual weight is substantial, but information points are few and focused
- **Medium-density page (secondary education/student presentations)**: Page title + left-side diagram or chart (occupying 40% of page) + right-side definition + 4 bullet points + bottom thought question card. Text and visuals each occupy roughly half; moderate information volume
- **Medium-high density page (higher education/corporate training)**: Page title + multi-zone content layout (e.g., left-image right-text, top-bottom columns) + definition + 5-6 bullet points + supplementary notes + case card or data table. Page content area is well-filled, but readability maintained through zoning and spacing

## Text-to-Visual Ratio: Visual Aid Primary

- Early education (kindergarten/elementary): approximately 20-30% text + 50-60% images/illustrations + 15-20% whitespace
- Secondary education: approximately 40% text + 40% charts/diagrams + 20% whitespace
- Higher education/corporate training: approximately 45-50% text + 35-40% charts/diagrams + 10-15% whitespace
- Corporate training: approximately 40% text + 45% charts/flowcharts/comparison diagrams + 15% whitespace
- Abstract concepts must be paired with visual explanations (flowcharts, diagrams, analogies)
- Formula/code pages focus on content but should still include diagrams or examples for comprehension support

## Color Guidance

- **Clarity and readability as the first principle**, ensuring sufficient contrast between text and background in projection/large-screen environments
- Kindergarten, elementary: Primarily bright, lively tones; multi-color combinations allowed to create an approachable atmosphere
- Middle school: Colors balance vitality and focus
- University/lectures: Steady primary color + one accent color for highlighting key points
- Student classroom presentations: Clean and unified
- All scenarios: Content pages primarily use white/light backgrounds; dark backgrounds only for cover/chapter/emphasis pages
- Knowledge categories can use color coding (e.g., "concept = blue," "case = green," "caution = orange"); no more than 4 semantic colors throughout
- **Strictly prohibited**: Large dark-background content pages (unreadable on projectors)

## Font Guidance

- **Titles**: Sans-serif Bold, clear and prominent
- **Body text**: Sans-serif Regular, ensuring readability in large classrooms/conference room projections
- Font size tiers (by audience):
  - Kindergarten: Cover title 44-56px → Page title 34-40px → Body text 22-26px (use 26px when page text content is light, 24px for moderate, 22px for heavy; must not go below 22px) → Annotations 16-18px
  - Elementary: Cover title 42-52px → Page title 30-34px → Body text 18-22px (use 22px when page text content is light, 20px for moderate, 18px for heavy; must not go below 18px) → Annotations 14px
  - Middle school: Cover title 38-48px → Page title 28-32px → Body text 18-22px (use 22px when page text content is light, 20px for moderate, 18px for heavy; must not go below 18px) → Annotations 14-16px
  - University/lectures/student presentations: Cover title 36-44px → Page title 26-30px → Body text 18-22px (use 22px when page text content is light, 20px for moderate, 18px for heavy; must not go below 18px) → Annotations 12-16px
  - Corporate training: Cover title 36-44px → Page title 26-30px → Body text 18-22px (use 22px when page text content is light, 20px for moderate, 18px for heavy; must not go below 18px) → Annotations 12-16px; safety/warning information should use enlarged and bolded font sizes
- Formulas rendered with LaTeX; code uses monospace fonts

## Narrative Style: Guided Teaching

- Each page title summarizes the knowledge point conclusion, not a vague chapter name
- Knowledge unfolds in sequence: Introduce problem/phenomenon → Concept explanation → Examples/cases → Summary/practice
- Use questions appropriately to provoke thought (e.g., "Why is the sky blue?" → next page provides the explanation)
- Use analogies to lower comprehension barriers for complex concepts (e.g., "Electric current is like water flow; voltage is like water pressure")
- Adjust language style by audience:
  - Kindergarten/elementary: Conversational, fun, encouraging ("Did you notice?" "Great job!")
  - Middle school: Guiding + moderate technical terms, balancing fun and depth
  - University/lectures: Technical terms + clear explanations, logically rigorous
  - Student presentations: Natural expression, logically clear; avoid being too casual or too academic
  - Corporate training: Practice-oriented, clear steps, concise language; use imperative tone for safety/compliance topics ("must," "strictly prohibited")

## Content Expression Techniques

- **Diagrams/flowcharts (core)**: Build concept relationship diagrams and process step diagrams using shape (rect/ellipse/roundRect) + line (straightConnector1 + arrow) + text. Do not use image placeholders; use real shapes. Light-colored background rect as the diagram area base; different colors distinguish input/output/core concepts
- **Comparison tables**: Concept comparisons, method comparisons with clear rows and columns; differences highlighted with color or bold
- **Right vs. wrong comparison**: Left-right columns showing correct and incorrect practices, paired with green/red visual indicators; suitable for standards instruction and operational training. Light-colored background cards (roundRect) wrap case content, distinguishing from body text area
- **Keyword emphasis**: Core terms/concepts bolded or accent-colored on first appearance
- **Numbered steps**: Operational procedures, problem-solving steps, etc. in numbered lists paired with diagrams
- **Checklists**: Key processes paired with checklist-style bullet lists for trainees to follow along

## Decoration Prohibitions

| Prohibited | Alternative |
|--------|---------|
| Decorative images unrelated to knowledge | Diagrams/illustrations that aid understanding |
| Dense text walls (>6 lines of body text) | Split across pages or replace with visual explanations |
| Flashy visual effects | Clean and restrained; content is the priority |
| Low-contrast color schemes | Ensure readability in projection environments |
| Overly small font sizes (<12px; <18px for early education) | Font sizes appropriate for classroom/conference room projection |
| Pure text listing of concepts | Supplement with diagrams, tables, comparisons |
| Long blocks of policy/regulation text in corporate training | Extract key points + case illustrations; put full text in appendix |
